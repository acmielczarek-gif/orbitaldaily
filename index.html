Worth a look tonight.

A C6.1 solar flare has recently fired from the Sun, and while tonight's Kp index sits at a modest 1.3, the quiet conditions present an opportunity rather than a disappointment. The real story is overhead: an astrophotography score of 5.9 out of 10 combined with only 15 percent cloud cover creates genuine shooting conditions for wide-field work, lunar detail, and deep-sky imaging. The Last Quarter Moon at 56 percent illumination will set early enough to leave the latter half of the night genuinely dark, and those clear skies are rare enough to warrant serious use. Meanwhile, the recent solar activity hints at possibilities: space weather can shift, and monitoring conditions through the night may reveal unexpected auroral activity at higher latitudes.

Get outside and stay there. For astrophotographers, the moon's position and the cloud forecast suggest a solid window for capturing the terminator in detail or pursuing galaxies and nebulae after midnight. For general observers, bring binoculars and a star chart to hunt the Perseid radiant in advance of the shower's peak in five weeks, or simply track the current path of 2007 AA2 as it passes at 17.8 lunar distances, a comfortable approach that poses no impact risk but offers perspective on the minor bodies moving through near-Earth space.
